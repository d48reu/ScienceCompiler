Senolytics / Aging Executive Brief

Bottom line
The senolytics corpus does not support a simple “senolytics are good” story. It supports a more useful one: some senolytic agents improve important aging-related outcomes, but some contexts show null effects, tissue-specific harms, or sex-specific downsides.

What we analyzed
- 10 study summaries
- 16 refined claims
- mixed agent classes: D+Q, fisetin, navitoclax, mixed senolytic regimens

What stands out
Positive anchors
- D+Q improved physical function and lifespan in old mice.
- Fisetin extended lifespan and reduced senescence markers.
- D+Q reduced disc degeneration.
- D+Q reduced liver inflammation / HCC in Sod1 knockout mice.
- Fisetin improved arterial function.

Negative / caution anchors
- Navitoclax caused trabecular bone loss and impaired osteoblast-lineage function.
- D+Q mildly worsened obesity- and age-dependent liver disease progression in one model.
- D+Q did not improve overall influenza responses in aged mice.
- D+Q was detrimental in females in one sex-dimorphic study.

Interpretation
The category “senolytics” is too coarse. The evidence in this corpus suggests agent-specific and context-specific tradeoffs rather than a uniform class effect.

Best current thesis
Senolytic interventions appear promising but heterogeneous: benefits in lifespan, tissue maintenance, and resilience coexist with important harms, failures, and sex-dependent downsides, meaning senolytic efficacy should be framed as agent- and context-specific rather than universal.
